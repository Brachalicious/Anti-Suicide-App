🛟 Crisis Skills

These are evidence-based techniques used in crisis intervention. They work by changing your body chemistry, interrupting emotional spirals, and bringing you back to the present moment.

🧊 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding Technique
This technique brings you back to the present moment by engaging all your senses:
  • 5 things you can SEE - Look around and name them
  • 4 things you can TOUCH - Feel their texture
  • 3 things you can HEAR - Listen carefully
  • 2 things you can SMELL - Notice the scents
  • 1 thing you can TASTE - What's in your mouth?
💡 This works because it forces your brain to focus on the present instead of spiraling thoughts.
❄️ Ice & Cold Water (TIP Skill)
Temperature change activates your body's "dive reflex" - instantly calming your nervous system:
  • Hold ice cubes in your hands for 30-60 seconds
  • Splash ice-cold water on your face
  • Take a cold shower or bath
  • Put your face in a bowl of ice water (hold breath!)
💡 This physically slows your heart rate and reduces emotional intensity immediately.
🏃 Intense Exercise (Burn It Off)
Physical activity changes brain chemistry and releases built-up tension:
  • Run or sprint for 5-10 minutes
  • Do jumping jacks, burpees, or pushups
  • Dance hard to intense music
  • Punch a pillow or hit a punching bag
  • Go up and down stairs repeatedly
💡 Releases endorphins and burns off stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline.
📞 Reach Out & Connect
Human connection is one of the most powerful crisis tools:
  • Text or call a trusted friend or family member
  • You don't have to explain everything - just say "I'm having a hard time"
  • Call 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline - they're trained professionals
  • Text HOME to 741741 for Crisis Text Line
  • Join an online support community or chat
💡 Isolation makes everything worse. Connection reminds you that you're not alone.
⏰ Wait 15 Minutes (Ride the Wave)
Intense urges and emotions come in waves - they peak and then naturally decrease:
  • Set a 15-minute timer on your phone
  • Tell yourself "I just need to wait 15 minutes"
  • Watch a funny video, play a game, or scroll social media
  • By the time the timer goes off, the intensity will have decreased
  • If still intense, set another 15 minutes
💡 Urges typically peak at 10-15 minutes, then decrease naturally. You just need to outlast them.
✍️ Write It Out (Brain Dump)
Writing externalizes the pain and often brings unexpected clarity:
  • Grab paper and pen (or use your phone)
  • Write EVERYTHING you're feeling without filtering
  • Don't worry about grammar, spelling, or making sense
  • Let it all pour out - anger, sadness, confusion, everything
  • When done, you can tear it up or keep it
💡 Getting thoughts out of your head and onto paper creates distance and perspective.

⚠️ Know the Signs ▼

Critical Warning Signs (Act Immediately)

If you see any of these, call 988 or Hatzoloh (718-230-1000) right away:

  • Talking about wanting to die or kill themselves
  • Seeking methods for self-harm (searching online, obtaining weapons)
  • Talking about feeling hopeless or having no reason to live
  • Talking about being a burden to others

Warning Signs to Watch For

  • Withdrawal from friends, family, and activities they once enjoyed
  • Increased alcohol or drug use
  • Changes in sleep — sleeping too much or too little
  • Giving away prized or favorite possessions
  • Sudden mood changes — especially cheerfulness after depression
  • Persistent sadness, anxiety, or fatigue
  • Neglect of personal appearance or hygiene
  • Reckless or dangerous behavior
  • Expressing feelings of being trapped, hopeless, or worthless
  • Uncontrolled anger, hostility, or rage
  • Rushing to complete or revise a will
  • Frequent complaints of physical pain (headaches, stomachaches)

How to Help — Find the Words

  1. Start the conversation — Ask directly: "Are you thinking of ending your life?"
  2. Listen, express concern, reassure — Let them know you care and they are not alone
  3. Create a safety plan — Help identify warning signs, coping strategies, and people to call
  4. Get help — Call 988, go to an ER, or connect with a crisis counselor

Do NOT leave the person alone. Remove any lethal means (weapons, medications) from the area.

🛡️ Safety Planning ▼
�💙 After an Attempt ▼

Your journey of healing is one that many have been on and survived. Your life matters.

How Did I Get Here?

You may not understand all of the thoughts and feelings that led you to consider suicide, and that's okay. Many people who feel suicidal are experiencing a mental health concern, which is treatable.

While you may still have challenges, many people who survive a suicide attempt begin to see those challenges in a new light, and realize that there are people available to support them. You don't need to have all of the answers to heal from this experience. There is a way through.

Things You Can Do to Support Your Recovery

  1. Be kind to yourself. You have just survived a life-threatening health crisis and you deserve to take the time you need.
  2. Take care of your health. Exercising, eating right, getting enough sleep and spending time with healthy people can have a huge impact on your health and mood.
  3. Find a mental health professional. A good therapist or doctor can help you put this experience in proper perspective and develop a safety plan.
  4. Try a support group. A group can help you know you are not alone.
  5. Talk to those you trust. When you're ready, let them know what happened and that you want them to help you stay safe.

Safety Plan Essentials

  1. Recognize what puts you at risk
  2. Find coping strategies that do not rely on the presence of others
  3. Engage with people and go to places that help take your mind off your problems
  4. Reach out to family or friends that can help you in a crisis
  5. Call 988 for the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
  6. Keep your environment safe

Interacting with Family and Friends

Sometimes people do not know what to say following a suicide attempt. They may be frightened, confused, or angry, and say things that are not helpful to your recovery. They may also avoid discussing it with you.

They may need time to process what has happened. Their journey is not your journey, and you are not responsible for how they decide to work through their feelings. If asked about your attempt, tell people what you are comfortable telling them, or that you need time.

  • Harmful substances
  • Other means specific to your situation

Educational Resources

Understanding Depression

Depression is more than just feeling sad. It's a real medical condition that affects how you think, feel, and handle daily activities. It is treatable, and most people who receive treatment get better.

Anxiety & Stress Management

Anxiety is a normal reaction to stress, but when it becomes excessive, it may be a disorder. Learning to manage stress through healthy coping mechanisms can make a significant difference.

Building Resilience

Resilience is the ability to bounce back from setbacks. It can be developed through strong relationships, self-care, finding purpose, embracing healthy thoughts, and seeking help when needed.

Self-Care Practices

Regular exercise, adequate sleep, healthy eating, staying connected with loved ones, and setting healthy boundaries are all essential parts of maintaining good mental health.

Hospitals & IOPs

If you need a higher level of care, these options provide structured treatment programs and 24/7 support. Intensive Outpatient Programs (IOPs) offer flexible, focused therapy while you continue daily life.

Finding a Psychiatric Hospital

Psychiatric hospitals provide 24/7 inpatient care for individuals experiencing severe mental health crises. If you or someone you know is in immediate danger, call 988 or Hatzoloh (718-230-1000).

Intensive Outpatient Programs (IOPs)

IOPs provide structured therapy sessions (typically 3-5 days per week, 3+ hours per day) while allowing you to live at home. They're ideal for people who need more support than weekly therapy but don't require 24/7 inpatient care.

Partial Hospitalization Programs (PHPs)

PHPs offer full-day treatment programs (5-7 days per week) that provide intensive therapy, medication management, and skills training. You return home each evening, bridging the gap between inpatient care and IOPs.

Crisis Stabilization Units

Short-term residential programs (typically 24-72 hours) designed to stabilize individuals in acute mental health crisis. They offer a safe environment with clinical support as an alternative to emergency rooms.

How to Choose the Right Level of Care

Inpatient/Hospital: For immediate safety concerns, severe symptoms, or when you can't care for yourself.
PHP: For significant symptoms that need daily professional support but you're safe at home.
IOP: For moderate symptoms, stepping down from higher care, or when you need more than weekly therapy.
Outpatient: For ongoing maintenance, mild-to-moderate symptoms, and regular check-ins.
