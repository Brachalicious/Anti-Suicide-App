🛟 Crisis Skills

These are evidence-based techniques used in crisis intervention. They work by changing your body chemistry, interrupting emotional spirals, and bringing you back to the present moment.

🧊 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding Technique
This technique brings you back to the present moment by engaging all your senses:
  • 5 things you can SEE - Look around and name them
  • 4 things you can TOUCH - Feel their texture
  • 3 things you can HEAR - Listen carefully
  • 2 things you can SMELL - Notice the scents
  • 1 thing you can TASTE - What's in your mouth?
💡 This works because it forces your brain to focus on the present instead of spiraling thoughts.
❄️ Ice & Cold Water (TIP Skill)
Temperature change activates your body's "dive reflex" - instantly calming your nervous system:
  • Hold ice cubes in your hands for 30-60 seconds
  • Splash ice-cold water on your face
  • Take a cold shower or bath
  • Put your face in a bowl of ice water (hold breath!)
💡 This physically slows your heart rate and reduces emotional intensity immediately.
🏃 Intense Exercise (Burn It Off)
Physical activity changes brain chemistry and releases built-up tension:
  • Run or sprint for 5-10 minutes
  • Do jumping jacks, burpees, or pushups
  • Dance hard to intense music
  • Punch a pillow or hit a punching bag
  • Go up and down stairs repeatedly
💡 Releases endorphins and burns off stress hormones like cortisol and adrenaline.
📞 Reach Out & Connect
Human connection is one of the most powerful crisis tools:
  • Text or call a trusted friend or family member
  • You don't have to explain everything - just say "I'm having a hard time"
  • Call 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline - they're trained professionals
  • Text HOME to 741741 for Crisis Text Line
  • Join an online support community or chat
💡 Isolation makes everything worse. Connection reminds you that you're not alone.
⏰ Wait 15 Minutes (Ride the Wave)
Intense urges and emotions come in waves - they peak and then naturally decrease:
  • Set a 15-minute timer on your phone
  • Tell yourself "I just need to wait 15 minutes"
  • Watch a funny video, play a game, or scroll social media
  • By the time the timer goes off, the intensity will have decreased
  • If still intense, set another 15 minutes
💡 Urges typically peak at 10-15 minutes, then decrease naturally. You just need to outlast them.
✍️ Write It Out (Brain Dump)
Writing externalizes the pain and often brings unexpected clarity:
  • Grab paper and pen (or use your phone)
  • Write EVERYTHING you're feeling without filtering
  • Don't worry about grammar, spelling, or making sense
  • Let it all pour out - anger, sadness, confusion, everything
  • When done, you can tear it up or keep it
💡 Getting thoughts out of your head and onto paper creates distance and perspective.

🗣️ About Scream Therapy

Screaming to cope, often called scream therapy or primal therapy, acts as a cathartic release for pent-up stress, anger, and anxiety, triggering endorphin release and reducing cortisol. It helps regulate the nervous system by engaging the vagus nerve, providing temporary relief by allowing a safe outlet for emotional tension.

✨ Benefits:

  • Acts as a quick, temporary emotional reset
  • Helps lower blood pressure and heart rate after the initial shock
  • Releases repressed emotions and helps process trauma
  • Triggers endorphin release and reduces cortisol

🛡️ How to Do It Safely:

  • Shout into a pillow to muffle sound
  • Scream in your car with windows up
  • Use soundproof spaces to avoid frightening others
  • Keep sessions short (a few minutes)
  • Warm up your vocal cords beforehand

⚠️ Important Considerations:

  • Limited scientific evidence for long-term efficacy
  • Can sometimes trigger anxiety or fear by activating the limbic system
  • May damage vocal cords if done too frequently or intensely
  • Not a substitute for traditional therapy to address root causes of stress

💜 While some find it beneficial for immediate relief, please consider professional support for long-term healing.

💜 Benefits of Scream Therapy

Scream therapy, or primal therapy, provides immediate emotional relief by allowing you to release pent-up anger, stress, and trauma through intense vocalization. It acts as a cathartic tool that can lower cortisol, trigger endorphin release (similar to exercise), and reduce physical tension in the body.

Key Benefits:

  • Immediate Emotional Catharsis: Provides a safe outlet to "let it all out," offering a sense of lightness, freedom, and relief
  • Stress & Anxiety Reduction: Reduces bodily tension and lowers cortisol levels
  • Endorphin Boost: Triggers the release of feel-good neurotransmitters like dopamine and endorphins
  • Processing Trauma: Helps access and release deep-seated pain from childhood trauma or grief
  • Vagus Nerve Regulation: Loud, intentional vocalization can activate the vagus nerve, helping regulate the nervous system and lower heart rate
  • Empowerment: Allows you to speak up and reclaim your voice, especially if you've been in suppressing environments

Important: While many report benefits, research is limited. Treat this as a form of expression rather than a cure for mental health conditions. It may not be suitable for those with high blood pressure or heart conditions.

⚠️ This is NOT a substitute for professional therapy. If you feel like self-harming immediately, please call Hatzoloh at 718-230-1000 or the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline at 988.

⚡ About Silent Screaming

Silent screaming—or screaming without sound—acts as a powerful, non-verbal emotional release that can immediately reduce stress, calm the nervous system, and help manage anxiety, trauma, or overwhelming grief.

Mental and Physiological Effects:

  • Nervous System Regulation: Activates the vagus nerve, helping break up emotional "stuckness" stored in the body
  • Cathartic Release: Allows primal reaction to relieve repressed rage, anger, or frustration without noise
  • Managing High Stress: Acts as a grounding technique to process PTSD, panic, and anxiety
  • Reduced Inhibition: Can be done quietly anywhere, allowing immediate, uninhibited emotional expression

Important: Silent screaming can bring up a lot of emotions, especially for those who have had to stay silent for far too long and silently screamed for years. Use this in a safe place where you can switch between regular and silent screaming if needed. Grounding exercises are available between sessions.

⚠️ Know the Signs ▼

Critical Warning Signs (Act Immediately)

If you see any of these, call 988 or Hatzoloh (718-230-1000) right away:

  • Talking about wanting to die or kill themselves
  • Seeking methods for self-harm (searching online, obtaining weapons)
  • Talking about feeling hopeless or having no reason to live
  • Talking about being a burden to others

Warning Signs to Watch For

  • Withdrawal from friends, family, and activities they once enjoyed
  • Increased alcohol or drug use
  • Changes in sleep — sleeping too much or too little
  • Giving away prized or favorite possessions
  • Sudden mood changes — especially cheerfulness after depression
  • Persistent sadness, anxiety, or fatigue
  • Neglect of personal appearance or hygiene
  • Reckless or dangerous behavior
  • Expressing feelings of being trapped, hopeless, or worthless
  • Uncontrolled anger, hostility, or rage
  • Rushing to complete or revise a will
  • Frequent complaints of physical pain (headaches, stomachaches)

How to Help — Find the Words

  1. Start the conversation — Ask directly: "Are you thinking of ending your life?"
  2. Listen, express concern, reassure — Let them know you care and they are not alone
  3. Create a safety plan — Help identify warning signs, coping strategies, and people to call
  4. Get help — Call 988, go to an ER, or connect with a crisis counselor

Do NOT leave the person alone. Remove any lethal means (weapons, medications) from the area.

🛡️ Safety Planning ▼
�💙 After an Attempt ▼

Your journey of healing is one that many have been on and survived. Your life matters.

How Did I Get Here?

You may not understand all of the thoughts and feelings that led you to consider suicide, and that's okay. Many people who feel suicidal are experiencing a mental health concern, which is treatable.

While you may still have challenges, many people who survive a suicide attempt begin to see those challenges in a new light, and realize that there are people available to support them. You don't need to have all of the answers to heal from this experience. There is a way through.

Things You Can Do to Support Your Recovery

  1. Be kind to yourself. You have just survived a life-threatening health crisis and you deserve to take the time you need.
  2. Take care of your health. Exercising, eating right, getting enough sleep and spending time with healthy people can have a huge impact on your health and mood.
  3. Find a mental health professional. A good therapist or doctor can help you put this experience in proper perspective and develop a safety plan.
  4. Try a support group. A group can help you know you are not alone.
  5. Talk to those you trust. When you're ready, let them know what happened and that you want them to help you stay safe.

Safety Plan Essentials

  1. Recognize what puts you at risk
  2. Find coping strategies that do not rely on the presence of others
  3. Engage with people and go to places that help take your mind off your problems
  4. Reach out to family or friends that can help you in a crisis
  5. Call 988 for the Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
  6. Keep your environment safe

Interacting with Family and Friends

Sometimes people do not know what to say following a suicide attempt. They may be frightened, confused, or angry, and say things that are not helpful to your recovery. They may also avoid discussing it with you.

They may need time to process what has happened. Their journey is not your journey, and you are not responsible for how they decide to work through their feelings. If asked about your attempt, tell people what you are comfortable telling them, or that you need time.

Educational Resources

Understanding Depression

Depression is more than just feeling sad. It's a real medical condition that affects how you think, feel, and handle daily activities. It is treatable, and most people who receive treatment get better.

Anxiety & Stress Management

Anxiety is a normal reaction to stress, but when it becomes excessive, it may be a disorder. Learning to manage stress through healthy coping mechanisms can make a significant difference.

Building Resilience

Resilience is the ability to bounce back from setbacks. It can be developed through strong relationships, self-care, finding purpose, embracing healthy thoughts, and seeking help when needed.

Self-Care Practices

Regular exercise, adequate sleep, healthy eating, staying connected with loved ones, and setting healthy boundaries are all essential parts of maintaining good mental health.

Hospitals & IOPs

If you need a higher level of care, these options provide structured treatment programs and 24/7 support. Intensive Outpatient Programs (IOPs) offer flexible, focused therapy while you continue daily life.

Finding a Psychiatric Hospital

Psychiatric hospitals provide 24/7 inpatient care for individuals experiencing severe mental health crises. If you or someone you know is in immediate danger, call 988 or Hatzoloh (718-230-1000).

Intensive Outpatient Programs (IOPs)

IOPs provide structured therapy sessions (typically 3-5 days per week, 3+ hours per day) while allowing you to live at home. They're ideal for people who need more support than weekly therapy but don't require 24/7 inpatient care.

Partial Hospitalization Programs (PHPs)

PHPs offer full-day treatment programs (5-7 days per week) that provide intensive therapy, medication management, and skills training. You return home each evening, bridging the gap between inpatient care and IOPs.

Crisis Stabilization Units

Short-term residential programs (typically 24-72 hours) designed to stabilize individuals in acute mental health crisis. They offer a safe environment with clinical support as an alternative to emergency rooms.

How to Choose the Right Level of Care

Inpatient/Hospital: For immediate safety concerns, severe symptoms, or when you can't care for yourself.
PHP: For significant symptoms that need daily professional support but you're safe at home.
IOP: For moderate symptoms, stepping down from higher care, or when you need more than weekly therapy.
Outpatient: For ongoing maintenance, mild-to-moderate symptoms, and regular check-ins.

🌀 Breaking the Stigma: Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)

DID (formerly known as Multiple Personality Disorder) is one of the most misunderstood and stigmatized mental health conditions. Movies and media have twisted it into something "scary" or "dangerous," but the reality is far different.

The Truth About DID:

  • DID is a trauma response — the brain's way of surviving unbearable pain
  • People with DID are NOT dangerous — they're often incredibly kind and empathetic
  • DID is REAL — it's recognized by medical professionals worldwide
  • People with DID deserve compassion, not fear
  • With proper treatment and support, people with DID can live fulfilling lives

📚 Mental Health Conditions Directory

Knowledge is power. Click on any condition to learn more and understand that ALL of these are REAL, VALID, and TREATABLE.

😰 Anxiety Disorders

▾

Common anxiety disorders include:

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
  • Panic Disorder
  • Social Anxiety Disorder
  • Specific Phobias
  • Agoraphobia
  • Separation Anxiety Disorder

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health conditions, affecting millions. They involve excessive worry, fear, or panic that interferes with daily life. Treatment includes therapy (especially CBT), medication, and lifestyle changes.

💙 Depressive Disorders

▾

Types of depression include:

  • Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
  • Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)
  • Postpartum Depression
  • Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)
  • Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD)

Depression is more than sadness—it's a serious medical condition affecting mood, energy, sleep, appetite, and ability to function. It's NOT a choice or weakness. Treatment works through therapy, medication, and lifestyle support.

🌀 Dissociative Disorders

▾

Dissociative disorders include:

  • Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)
  • Dissociative Amnesia
  • Depersonalization/Derealization Disorder

Dissociative disorders involve disconnection from thoughts, memories, feelings, or sense of identity, often as a response to trauma. They are REAL conditions that require specialized treatment.

🍽️ Eating Disorders

▾

Eating disorders include:

  • Anorexia Nervosa
  • Bulimia Nervosa
  • Binge Eating Disorder
  • Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID)
  • Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder (OSFED)

Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions involving disturbed eating patterns and body image. They have the highest mortality rate of any mental illness and require comprehensive medical and psychological treatment.

🎭 Mood Disorders

▾

Mood disorders include:

  • Bipolar I Disorder
  • Bipolar II Disorder
  • Cyclothymic Disorder
  • Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder (DMDD)

Mood disorders involve extreme mood swings between emotional highs (mania/hypomania) and lows (depression). They affect energy, activity levels, sleep, and judgment. Proper diagnosis and treatment are essential for managing these conditions.

🔄 Obsessive-Compulsive & Related

▾

These disorders include:

  • Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)
  • Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)
  • Hoarding Disorder
  • Trichotillomania (Hair-Pulling Disorder)
  • Excoriation Disorder (Skin-Picking)

These conditions involve intrusive thoughts (obsessions) and/or repetitive behaviors (compulsions) that significantly interfere with daily life. They are treatable with specialized therapy (like ERP) and medication.

🎭 Personality Disorders

▾

Personality disorders include:

  • Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)
  • Antisocial Personality Disorder
  • Narcissistic Personality Disorder
  • Avoidant Personality Disorder
  • Dependent Personality Disorder
  • Paranoid Personality Disorder
  • Schizoid Personality Disorder
  • Schizotypal Personality Disorder
  • Histrionic Personality Disorder
  • Obsessive-Compulsive Personality Disorder (OCPD)

Personality disorders involve enduring patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that differ significantly from cultural expectations and cause distress. With proper treatment (especially DBT for BPD), people can learn effective coping strategies and lead fulfilling lives.

🧠 Psychotic Disorders

▾

Psychotic disorders include:

  • Schizophrenia
  • Schizoaffective Disorder
  • Schizophreniform Disorder
  • Brief Psychotic Disorder
  • Delusional Disorder

Psychotic disorders involve distorted thinking and awareness, including hallucinations and delusions. They are serious but TREATABLE conditions. With proper medication and support, many people with psychotic disorders live full, productive lives.

💔 Trauma & Stressor-Related Disorders

▾

Trauma-related disorders include:

  • Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)
  • Complex PTSD (C-PTSD)
  • Acute Stress Disorder
  • Adjustment Disorders
  • Reactive Attachment Disorder

These conditions develop after exposure to traumatic or stressful events. They are NOT signs of weakness—they are normal reactions to abnormal circumstances. Trauma-focused therapies (like EMDR and CPT) are highly effective.

💡 Remember: These are MEDICAL conditions, not character flaws. Every single one is valid, real, and deserving of compassionate care. If you recognize yourself in any of these, please reach out for help. You deserve support.

📚 Learning Disabilities Support

Having a learning disability does NOT mean you're less intelligent. Your brain just processes information differently, and that's okay. You are capable, you are smart, and you deserve support and understanding.

🧠 What Are Learning Disabilities?

Learning disabilities are neurological differences that affect how your brain receives, processes, stores, and responds to information. They have NOTHING to do with intelligence or effort.

Common Types Include:

  • Dyslexia - Affects reading, writing, and spelling
  • Dyscalculia - Affects understanding numbers and math concepts
  • Dysgraphia - Affects writing and fine motor skills
  • Auditory Processing Disorder - Affects understanding spoken language
  • Visual Processing Disorder - Affects interpreting visual information
  • ADHD - Affects attention, focus, and impulse control
  • Executive Function Disorder - Affects planning, organization, and time management

🎥 You Are NOT Stupid - Watch This

This powerful message is for anyone who's ever been told they're "slow," "lazy," or "not smart enough." You need to hear this.

💚 Truth Bombs

✨ You are NOT stupid. Your brain is wired differently, which actually means you might excel at things others struggle with. Many successful people have learning disabilities!
✨ It's NOT your fault. You didn't cause this, and you're not lazy. Your brain processes information differently—that's neurology, not character.
✨ You CAN succeed. With the right accommodations, strategies, and support, people with learning disabilities achieve incredible things. You just need the right tools.
✨ You deserve accommodations. Extra time, audio textbooks, assistive technology—these aren't "cheating." They're leveling the playing field so your intelligence can shine through.
✨ Your worth isn't measured by grades. Intelligence comes in many forms. You might struggle with reading but excel at problem-solving, creativity, or emotional intelligence.

🛡️ Bullying Support

If you're being bullied, you need to know this: It's NOT your fault. You do NOT deserve it. And you are NOT alone. This section is your survival guide, your validation, and your way forward.

❤️ First: You Need to Hear This

🔴 This is NOT your fault. Bullies target people for their own issues — insecurity, power, control. It's about THEM, not you.
🔴 You do NOT deserve this. No one deserves to be bullied. Ever. For any reason.
🔴 You are NOT weak for being affected. Bullying causes real psychological harm. Your feelings are valid.
🔴 You are NOT alone. 1 in 5 students report being bullied. It's devastatingly common — and we're here to help you through it.

🔍 What IS Bullying?

Bullying is repeated, intentional harm. It includes:

Physical: Hitting, pushing, tripping, destroying belongings
Verbal: Name-calling, insults, threats, mocking
Social: Exclusion, spreading rumors, public humiliation, isolation
Cyberbullying: Online harassment, hate messages, posting embarrassing content, impersonation

If someone is repeatedly making you feel scared, worthless, or unsafe — that's bullying. Trust your gut.

🚨 Immediate Safety Steps

  1. Get to safety. If you're in physical danger, get away NOW. Go to a teacher, security, a store, a trusted adult.
  2. Document everything. Screenshot messages. Write down dates, times, what happened, who was there. Evidence matters.
  3. Tell a trusted adult. Parent, teacher, counselor, rabbi, coach — someone who will take you seriously.
  4. Block and report online bullies. On every platform. Don't engage — block, report, move on.
  5. DO NOT retaliate. It can make things worse and get YOU in trouble. Let adults handle it.
  6. If adults don't help, tell another adult. Keep telling until someone listens. You deserve protection.

💪 Survival Strategies

🔹 The "Gray Rock" Method

Be as boring and uninteresting as possible. Bullies feed on reactions. Give them NOTHING. Short answers. Neutral face. Walk away.

🔹 Stay in Groups

Bullies usually target people who are alone. Stick with friends, sit with others at lunch, walk with someone in the hallways.

🔹 Change Your Routine

If bullies wait for you in certain places, take a different route. Use different bathrooms. Sit in different spots.

🔹 Practice Confident Body Language

Stand tall. Make eye contact. Speak clearly. Even if you're terrified inside, LOOK confident. Bullies target perceived weakness.

🔹 Find Your People

Join clubs, teams, groups where you feel accepted. Online communities count too. You need safe spaces where you belong.

📱 For Cyberbullying Specifically

  • SCREENSHOT EVERYTHING. Before you block them. Evidence is crucial.
  • Block immediately. Don't read their messages. Don't respond. Block and move on.
  • Report to the platform. Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, etc. — they all have reporting systems.
  • Make your accounts private. Limit who can contact you and see your posts.
  • Don't share personal info online. School name, location, phone number — keep it private.
  • Take a social media break if needed. Your mental health > social media. It's okay to log off.
  • If it's severe, contact police. Threats, blackmail, sexual content — that's criminal. Report it.
